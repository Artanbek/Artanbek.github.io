ABSTRACT

In this paper, two algorithms for a post-quantum cryptography (PQC) scheme were presented to close the security gap created by quantum computer attacks on our current public-key cryptosystems and to solve the issue of key sharing peculiar to symmetric algorithms.

A substitution cipher known as homophonic encryption (HE) of the polyalphabetic variety that completely defeats frequency analysis was reviewed here. The findings show that it is well suited for protecting databases against known computer threats. The other algorithm studied here was identity-based encryption (IBE), a unique public-key algorithm with many properties well suited for key management and data exchange.

As recommended by security experts, hybrid cryptography should have an approved quantum-resistant public-key algorithm deployed alongside the traditional ones (prequantum algorithms) to make it not less secure than PQC. So, a dual-layer crypto scheme is considered here, consisting of a homophonic cipher for encrypting databases and a variant of the IBE algorithm already accepted for PQC to secure data exchange across networks.

The experiment focused on bringing HE into the spotlight and was conducted in Cryptool software to find the throughput rates for homophonic encryption and decryption. And the Friedman frequency test was performed on a dataset to validate how homophonic encryption beats cryptanalysis.

Chapter 1.0    INTRODUCTION

1.1 The Problem

1.1.1  Offensive cyberattack concerns

With the advent of quantum computers, a new set of security concerns have emerged. Peter Shor in 1994 developed a polynomial-time algorithm for factoring integers amazingly fast. When implemented as a fast Fourier transform (FFT) in a classical computer circuit, it requires O(n2n) gates, which is an exponential quantity. However, the decomposition in the quantum Fourier transform (QFT) with 2n amplitudes, uses O(n2) Hadamard gates so only O (n log n) gates are needed to achieve an effective approximation. (Gerjuoy, 2005). This is a considerable speedup in quadratic factorization! Factorization is the backbone of our current interconnection, which uses asymmetric key techniques such as the Digital Signature Algorithm (DSA), Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm (ECDSA), and RivestShamirAdleman (RSA). With the significant advances in quantum approaches to large-scale factorization using Shor Algorithm, these schemes are no longer considered secure.

Because of the threat posed by the Shor factoring algorithm, most existing asymmetric key algorithms that rely on factorization now require longer key lengths than their symmetric counterparts to remain secure. For example, a 1024-bit key in terms of security strength in the asymmetric RSA, is now equivalent to an 80-bit key in the symmetric Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)! Further scaling the already large key size of asymmetric key algorithms is a major challenge because the huge overhead degrades the algorithm's performance. Still, it can eventually be cracked by quantum computer attacks.

Grover algorithm is another quantum algorithm that provides a quadratic speedup in searching a database by using O(?N) evaluations for the function that symmetric key algorithms are vulnerable to. However, unlike asymmetric key algorithms, symmetric key algorithms perform better overall against quantum computer attacks and are efficient due to the minimal overhead cost for performance incurred by the comparatively small key size. For example, most symmetric key algorithms from 256 bits upward are considered quantum secure. However, symmetric key algorithms face problems in managing key sharing between sender and receiver because they are a one-key cipher, unlike asymmetric key algorithms that use two keys, one public and one private, making them more suitable for key sharing and data exchange.

1.1.2  Defensive cybersecurity solutions

Given the threat to our critical infrastructures from quantum computing attacks, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) launched a Post-Quantum Cryptography Standardization (PQS) drive in 2017. The campaigns goal is for security specialists, enthusiasts, and the public to increase the strength of current algorithms, where possible, and to develop new algorithms resistant to quantum computing attacks. This will involve using a PQS metric to measure the quantum security safety of algorithms. (NIST, 2017).

1.3 The Scope

1.3.1  What is Cryptography?

Cryptography deals with encrypting and decrypting messages to ensure secure communications and that our data assets are protected to maintain confidentiality, integrity, and availability (CIA).

 Encryption is a process whereby plaintexts are converted to meaningless ciphertext, while the decryption process converts ciphertext back to plaintext. To perform encryption and decryption, the communicating parties need a piece of information called a key, which must be generated before encryption. The key is a vital component that governs the operation of a cryptosystem by determining how plaintext is converted into ciphertext and how it can be decrypted later.

Figure 1: Encryption Scheme

When an algorithm combines encryption, decryption, and key generation, we call this an encryption scheme. We have two main schemas: secret-key cryptography (symmetric) and public-key cryptography (asymmetric). Keys are employed in cryptographic algorithms for security purposes, such as digital signature methods and message authentication codes.

 When both parties use a single key for encrypting and decrypting messages, we call it a symmetric cipher. In asymmetric encryption, two keys related to mathematical methods are generated to decrypt messages. The public key is issued to any party wishing to communicate with the owner to encrypt the messages safely. The key owner then uses their private key, always kept secret, to decrypt the messages. The mathematical relationship between public and private keys is a computationally hard challenge for an adversary to deduce and decrypt a ciphertext with only knowledge of the public key.

1.3.2  Cryptanalysis?

The study of cryptography (ciphering and deciphering) and cryptanalysis (methods for obtaining encrypted information without knowing the secret keys) stands as the basis of cryptology. Cryptanalysis, a discipline related to cryptography, is the study of the vulnerabilities and leaks in the implementation of cryptographic systems, and a review of the mathematical concepts that underlie them. The result of cryptanalysis, which is also touted as a means of cracking encryption, can provide someone with the knowledge of how to circumvent the purpose of that encryption. In such a scenario, there could be breaches of data, loss of privacy, or even damages.

The followings are some types of cryptographic attacks, just to name a few:

Brute-force attack (BFA): here the attacker tries all possible keys to guess the right key.

Differential fault analysis (DFA): here the attacker looks for any relevant information in the output resulting from errors induced in the cryptosystem.

Frequency analysis: This is based on specific letter combinations and text occurrences that occur with varied frequencies in a language.

Man-in-middle-attack (MIM): happens mostly by eavesdropping on public key cryptosystems where key exchange occurs before communication.

Side channel attack (SCA): where the attacker exploits a flaw in the physical implementation of a cryptosystem algorithm. (Gobi & Kurma, 2018).

1.3.3  What is Post Quantum Cryptography?

Post-quantum cryptography refers to cryptographic techniques that are very resistant to quantum computer attacks. If a powerful quantum computer is employed against the hard-mathematical problems of our current public key schemes that depend either on integer factorization, or the discrete logarithm of elliptic-curves, it can be cracked. In his groundbreaking research, Shor showed that a quantum computer can effectively solve both problems (Kaliski, 2017). Although such a powerful quantum computer has yet to be publicly deployed, it is under development. This has fueled the race to develop robust algorithms to defend against the anticipated attacks. On the other hand, Grover algorithm speedup search against symmetric algorithms is good but doubling the key size can effectively stop the attacks from succeeding (ENISA et al., 2022).

1.3.4  Identity-Based Encryption

ID-based encryption, also known simply as identity-based encryption (IBE), uses a public key technique to encrypt messages with info about the user's identity (for example, their official email address, tax code, et cetera). This means that a sender with access to a trusted central authority for the system can encrypt a message using, for example, the official email address of the receiver. The receiver gets the encrypted message and decrypts it with their own private key.

 The IBE has a trusted third-party algorithm called Private Key Generator (PKG), which has a master public key and a master private key. This is the trusted central authority of the system. At the beginning of operations, the master public key must be revealed to the intended recipients, but the master private key is kept secret by the PKG. Next, the recipients must request their own private key from the PKG, associated with their IDs. The recipient's identity can be their email address, e.g., client@cloud.com, or any ASCII string, like their tax number. Recipients use their private keys to decrypt encrypted messages sent to them by any sender.

Once the authorization ID, such as their email address, is known, the sender adds it to the master public key to generate the receiver's public key, which will then be used to encrypt the message sent to the recipient. In contrast, the recipient can decrypt the messages using their own private key associated with their ID.  

This encryption method is so effective because interested parties only need to know information about the recipient ID, such as their official email address, rather than the key itself, to send them secure messages. As a result, IBE is particularly good for data exchange in a networked or corporate setting. A good reason to use this encryption is to set up public key infrastructure management, which is also suitable for managing databases that require much collaboration between users and data sharing across networks.

Another wonderful feature of the IBE scheme is that the keys expiration date can be set using the client encrypted messages sent with the public key: client@cloud.com || current year. This allows the private key to be made valid only for that year. However, if the encrypted message for the email is client@cloud.com || current date, the client is forced to get a new private key every day. The key can be revoked if the employee stops working for that organization. This is how key revocation is managed in the IBE. (Boneh & Franklin, 2003).

An IBE scheme is divided into four sets of algorithms:

SETUP: A set of system parameters P, the message space M and ciphertext space C, master public-key Kpub and master private-key Kpriv

GENERATE: This algorithm assigns a private key to a user by the PKG, when requested. It uses an input P, Kpriv and the identifier ID ? {0,1}* and returns a privatekey d for the user ID

ENCRYPT: It uses P, message m ? M and ID ? {0,1}* to give encryption c ? C

DECRYPT: Takes d, P and c ? C to give m ? M

1.3.5  Substitution Ciphers

Substitution ciphers replace plaintext with other text letters.

Monoalphabetic Substitution Cipher: Substitution for a letter is always by the same ciphertext letter. The number of the plaintext and ciphertext symbols, respectively, are 26 letters. (This cipher for letters can be broken because the sample space is limited to 262 = 676 maximum combinations).

Homophonic Substitution (for letter): More than one letter is used to replace a letter. Here the number of plaintext symbols are 26 letters. However, that of the ciphertext is greater than 26. (This cipher for letters can still be broken with more advanced analysis).

Polyalphabetic Substitution (for letter): The alphabet is switched after encrypting a letter, resulting in distinct ciphertext alphabets. (This cipher may still be broken with more advanced analysis).

Polyalphabetic Homophonic Substitution (for text): Whole text, like words and phrases, is replaced by unique tokens used only once, known as homophones. (This cipher cannot be broken because of its absolute secrecy). (Kopal et al., 2018).

2.1 History of Cryptography

To better understand the PQC described in this study, the following background literature on computer history and cryptography linked to the thesis issue and its dynamics have been reviewed. Because of its strength, the classical substitution cipher was once dubbed "The Great Cipher" (nomenclator) when it first appeared centuries ago. Vernam Cipher was chosen for review because it is an unbreakable cipher with similar principles to homophonic techniques to evade frequency analysis based on repetition.

 The Caesar Cipher

The Caesar cipher in the 1st century B.C. was notable from the Romans. The letters in words are agreed in advance to be shifted by some character distance. Decoding it could be challenging, especially if the rearrangement patterns are randomized. Therefore, Caesar cipher is called shift cipher because characters are shifted out of positions. A substitution cipher like this is intended to shift, reverse, or rotate the characters. Below is an example of randomly shifting alphabets in advance:

Plaintext alphabet:   ABCDEFGHIJKLMNOPQRSTUVWXYZ

Ciphertext alphabet:  ZEBRASCOLDFUNGHIJKMPQTVWXY

This method can be analyzed and cracked using frequency analysis because the letters are only substituted by shifting the positions. Frequency analysis helps us determine the letters that occur so often in collected datasets called corpuses (Piantadosi, 2014). For example, e is the most frequently used letters in English and z is the least. Some frequently used words used in English very often in descending order are: the, of, and, to, a, is, on, that, by, this, with, I, you, and it.

2.1.2  The Great Cipher (Nomenclator)

The ciphers Mary Queen of Scots employed to communicate information to her accomplices against Queen Elizabeth I of England until she was captured are a good example of 16th-century encryption. Mary utilized a "nomenclator cipher," which uses codes to change words and letters of the alphabet listed in a book. It was exceptionally difficult to crack because of the apparent randomness and well-thought-out traps at times. The Great Cipher is a nomenclator cipher established by the Rossignols family in 1600 A.D. that no one could break for a long time and to some extent even now. It is also called a "substitution cipher." tienne Bazeries broke a handful of the ciphers over three years in 1893 when he correctly identified what one pattern indicated. (The National Archives, n.d.).

Figure 3: The Nomenclator Codebook

He then concluded that the French syllable was being replaced instead of the letters, which was unlike usual ciphers of the time. Even then, the cipher was carefully designed to avoid frequency analysis. The most common French vowel, "e," for example, has 131 of the total 711 code digits utilized by the Rossignols family nomenclator codebook! Unfortunately, even after Bazeries cracked The Great Cipher, no one has cracked it again since then, so a substantial portion of the encrypted writings in the French Archives are lost.

2.1.3  Vernam Cipher

The invention of the one-time pad is attributed to Gilbert Vernam, who obtained an AT&T (American Telephone and Telegraph Company) patent for it in 1919. It is also referred to as the Vernam cipher. (Bellovin, 2011).

 It is an encryption technique that is provably secure and cannot be cracked. If someone who accesses a ciphertext cannot have any clue about the plaintext without knowing the key, it is said by Shannon that the encryption has perfect secrecy.

In cryptography, the one-time pad (OTP) is a technique of encryption that is uncrackable because it requires that a pre-shared key be used only one-time that is the same size or even longer than the message sent. This type of encryption encrypts plaintext with a random secret key.

The key must be random,

be the same in length or longer than the plaintext,

it should be used only once,

kept utterly secret,

and only then is the ciphertext guaranteed to be uncrackable.

It has also been proven that any key with perfectly secret cipher properties must use the same keys requirements as that of OTP keys.

OTP is difficult to use since the password can become extremely long if it encrypts a lengthy message. Sumathy (2020) reviewed OTP in mobile authentication and transaction security. The evolution of technology makes smartphones and PDAs (personal digital assistance) more complex. A stepped-down version of OTP (one-time password) with key that may not be as long as the message it encrypts, is used on mBanking with the assumption that if it is used only once and designed to expire quickly, it is still very difficult to crack. The bank usually sends it by SMS to the registered mobile number. To complete the transaction, the client must send the OTP back on time. This is done to authenticate the user and avoid any phishing or man-in-the-middle attacks.

2.1.4  Frequency Smoothing Encryption (FSE)

It took work to find information on research about homophonic encryption of whole texts since most works on homophonic encryption dealt only with the discussion and cryptanalysis of crackable monoalphabetic and (two-letter) polyalphabetic substitutions. The researcher had to request this information from cryptography specialist Paul Grubbs, an assistant professor at the University of Michigan, who sent him a copy of his colleagues Lacharit and Paterson's research paper on HE for whole texts.

Their research found that HE is an unbreakable cipher for whole texts because each text must be replaced by homophones (unique tokens), flattening the frequency distribution of messages. However, the cost becomes high when large amounts of data are encrypted. To improve HE efficiency, a compromise had to be found for the reuse of homophones. They came up with the idea of redesigning HE into a frequency smoothing algorithm (FSE) that uses homophones to target the frequency of texts. Furthermore, they find a use case in databases where this strategy provides optimal security.

Example on frequency targeting: if text messages m1, m2, and m3 occur in a frequency ratio of 1:2:3 in a sample, M, they can be substituted by six homophones in the same ratio of 1:2:3. After encoding 30 words, its possible to find that the unique homophones occur evenly, five times each.

In this way, it was acceptable to reuse homophones without affecting security, if they were deployed proportionally to the frequency target of m ? M. Given that the message distribution for the tokens will appear equally for each intended frequency of m ? M targeted, flattening their curves, the ciphered data can contain repetitions of homophones. In databases, if each column is encrypted with FSE and entries in neighboring rows are not associated, there was no vulnerability caught in all the cryptanalysis test cases performed on it. Because the message distributions are seen to be flattened by the frequency smoothing encryption technique, data privacy was obtained. The FSE approach was entirely algorithmic, therefore it was recognized as a workable balance between lowering frequency leakage and increasing query complexity by enhancing the speed of the FSE algorithm, provided that the databases are still well-secured against snapshot attackers. The permutational complexity also presented by not being able to sniff on all the distributed tables and then compare the unrelated rows to make sense of single tuples is an insurmountable obstacle to snapshot attackers. (Lacharit & Paterson, 2018).

2.1.5  IBE history and variants

Adi Shamir is credited with the concept of identity-based cryptography in 1984. Its main innovation was using user identities, such as email addresses or phone numbers for encryption and verification instead of digital certificates. However, he still needs to address the solution of implementing this in a system. (Shamir, 1984).

By eliminating the need to create and manage user certificates, IBE dramatically reduces the complexity of using certain cryptographic techniques. It also makes encryption easier for novices, since messages can be encrypted for users in the background before they interact with the system.

However, Dan Boneh and Matthew K. Franklin in 2001 proposed an IBE scheme based on bilinear pairings over elliptic curves and finite fields. (Boneh & Franklin, 2003). In the same year, Clifford Cocks proposed an IBE method based on the assumption of quadratic residuosity that encrypts messages one bit at a time (that requires 2log(n) cipher bits). This high amount of ciphertext expansion makes it inefficient. (Cooks, 2001).

Boneh, Gentry, and Hamburg presented an elegant solution for two IBE schemes BasicIBE and AnonIBE under the quadratic residuosity assumption. (Boneh et al., 2007). However, their method has a critical flaw: it employs a quartic time deterministic approach to compute solutions to quadratic bivariate congruences. Jhanwar and Barua improved on that by proposing a more efficient probabilistic technique in place of this deterministic one. However, their system, as well as various modifications, were recently shown to be vulnerable as well.

A new IBE approach based on the trapdoor discrete logarithm (TDL) problem was presented by Peterson and Srinivasan. It computes the discrete logarithm in the RSA modulus based on factorization complexity. The system setup and key extraction are too expensive in this scheme, despite its efficiency in encryption and decryption. (Paterson & Srinivasan, 2009).

Agrawal, Boneh, and Boyen introduced IBE techniques based on lattice-based cryptography (LBC); however, lattices are still more computationally intensive than modular exponentiation and bilinear pairing in terms of key extraction/generation. (Agrawal et al., 2010). However, this promises to be a robust system if an adaptively secure lattice-based IBE is developed in the standard model with a shorter extraction cost.

2.2 The Church Turing Thesis

We often credit the English mathematician, Alan Mathison Turing, to be the originator of the modern computer. Nevertheless, before his paper was published in 1936, the German mathematician David Hilbert in 1928 proposed a problem about the nature of mathematics by asking if there exists a general algorithm a mathematician can follow that may enable them to find out whether a given mathematical statement is provable. This was more like our traditional way of solving mathematics (on paper-and-pen algorithm) starting with a mathematical conjecture before we may proceed to the actual numbers involved, and then proving the algorithm was provable.

 Turing machine went a step further (to free us from this time-consuming manual effort) by stating that a single, universal programmable machine, could solve any algorithm whatsoever. The backbone of this principle lies in the total number of computational steps required to solve the algorithm problem (which is) independent of the underlying design of the computer. Furthermore, the basic principle, or the extended Church Turing thesis, indicates that a computational task can be resolved faster by reducing the time taken in implementing a single step, by performing several parallel steps, or reducing the total number of steps using a clever algorithm like FFT.

 Somehow the way quantum computers function violates the extended Church-Turing thesis because some computational problems are solved at exponentially fewer steps than the classical counterpart algorithm for the same job. Peter Shor demonstrated this, realistically, by developing quantum algorithms that factor big numbers and compute discrete logarithms exponentially faster than any classical computer.

The question we may ask here is Turings analysis incomplete? Or are quantum computers fundamentally different from their classical counterparts? Or is it something to do with what an algorithm is? In 1985, David Deutsch proposed a more concrete way to define an algorithm. He noted that though every algorithm is done on a physical system, either by writing down the math with paper-and-pen or via a computer, he asked if there is a universal computing device that will be able to simulate any other physical system efficiently. (Deutsch, 1985).

 Because algorithms must be performed on a physical system, Deutsch is pointing out that we do not need to justify the notion of algorithm, as Turing had done. We can use the laws of physics to prove that our device is truly universal. To answer this question, Deutsch had to invent a new computing system based on quantum physics, which can do everything classical computers can do, but is capable of simulating quantum physics; therefore, it is a more natural computing model.

2.2.1  Classical vs Quantum Computer

Classical computing can also be called binary computing because the information is stored as bits, 0 or 1. The first computer, Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer (ENIAC) from 1946, used a bulky charged vacuum tubes processor, which made it very slow. But with the advent of the first monolithic integrated circuit in 1958, Moores law was able to predict the steady growth in the number of transistors used and IC (integrated circuit) performance over the years.

 In 1979, Paul Benioff, a physicist, did proposed a quantum mechanical model of the Turing machine in a paper. In it he explained the concepts of quantum computing and stated that it was possible to build one. Then, in the early 1980s, Yuri Manin and Richard Feynman suggested building a quantum computer that could simulate things that a classical computer could not do.

 Quantum computer uses qubits, in full, quantum bits. Unlike the classical bits which are a determined 0 or 1, qubits are determined by their quantum physical states in every superposition state, which is a probabilistic 0 and or 1. The vectors ?0? and ?1? can be represented by the equation: 

|??= ??0? + ??1?

If the probability at instantiation to get a 0, or 1, is a half, the sum of the vector will be 1.

??j?? = 1 or |a|2 +|b|2 = 1

Qubits can be in a state of superposition ?0? and ?1?. By becoming entangled, two qubits in superposition can use both store of all four potential quantum state combinations in the qubits.

 Quantum qubits face a pressing problem: they can be very unstable at some depths during entanglement, so progress in material science and error correction codes must also be made to create reliable quantum circuits for use.

2.3 Why Post Quantum and not Quantum Cryptography?

Most people new to quantum computing need to be aware of the distinctions between post-quantum cryptography and quantum computer cryptography (QCC). Unfortunately, they are talking about different subjects. In the case of QCC, the algorithm must be a quantum algorithm or exhibit 'quantumness,' which means it must harness the qubit Hadamard gate features to exhibit quantum superimposition, entanglement, and so on.

There are numerous issues to consider while building quantum algorithms. It can be difficult because the programming language is still at the qubit machine level, such as QSKIT, and the quantum computer does not yet have a well-developed operating system, like classical computer. Even converting our current algorithms to qubit circuit registers for quantum computers is difficult, even if quantum computers can also run in classical mode. So, for the time being, the purpose of QCC is to construct quantum algorithms, which is still a sluggish process. PQC, on the other hand, is a type of cryptography that uses our current classical techniques to make it resistant to quantum computer attacks.

2.3.1  Quantum Key Distribution (QKD)

Due to key management issues, symmetric algorithms tend to be impractical as stand-alone methods and must be used in conjunction with public key cryptography to manage the key exchange. In addition, our current widely used asymmetric cryptography is vulnerable to attack due to advances in quantum computing. This made QKD the most secure way to distribute keys because it uses quantum technology directly. However, QKD is so dependent on infrastructure that it is currently impractical for large-scale deployment. The qubits must be transmitted via optic fiber cables and the roll out of the quantum cabled network is slow. That is why the technology is yet to be commercially available and we are considering post-quantum cryptography still using classical computer algorithms. So, we need to fall back on more innovative ways to manage our key issues, as key is an inseparable part of cryptography. Because of the infrastructural centric nature of QKD, it is better to consider it as quantum key distribution than as quantum cryptography, even though we are thinking about security.

In QKD an entangled quantum is transmitted between two parties. We would have two channels: the quantum channel to exchange the quantum qubits and a public classical channel to ascertain that no third-party is eavesdropping. Any measurements on the privately transmitted quanta by a third party would result in a modified quantum state indicated in the public reply to the communication, because a quantum state cannot be cloned arbitrarily without tampering with it, so the two parties concerned would discard the corrupted information. When estimating the quantity of information intercepted or lost across the quantum channel, the classical public channel would be utilized to authenticate the communication between the two parties and identify errors caused by eavesdropping. If the initial random qubits transmitted are deemed secure, the correct secret key distilled is accepted. Alternatively, else the entire process is repeated till safety is ensured.

2.3.2  Post Quantum Cryptography Standardization

PQS is an ongoing pipeline security project laid by NIST, that defines standards and requirements for cryptographic experts and enthusiasts, worldwide, to tap in. PQS helps us to classify current algorithms and new ones being published by metrics to resist quantum computer attacks. The last NIST organized PQC competition for algorithm and selection was based on:

Digital signature and

Public key encryption/exchange

There were about 100 submissions at the beginning. (NIST, 2019). Although no clear-cut winner emerged, the most promising submissions that reached the final included cryptosystems based mostly on lattices, isogenies, and hash functions like:

Classic McEliece: An asymmetric encryption technique that employs randomization in the encryption process. Recommended for "post-quantum cryptography" because it is resistant to attacks based on Shor's algorithm.

N-th degree Truncated polynomial Ring (NTRU): An open-source public-key cryptosystem that uses lattice-based encryption. It consists of two algorithms: NTRUEncrypt for encryption and NTRUSign for digital signatures. NTRU satisfy NIST security levels 1, 3, and 5.

Falcon: It Is a cryptographic signature algorithm submitted for PQS. It uses lattice-based signature. Falcon satisfy NIST security levels 1, 3, and 5.

Crystals-Kyber: It includes two cryptographic primitives, Kyber [a secure key encapsulation mechanism (KEM)], and Dilithium (a strongly secure digital signature) are both based on hard problems over module lattices that are resistant to large quantum computer assaults.

2.3.3  Six Security Level rankings for PQC proposed by NIST

NIST proposed that quantum security be evaluated the in following ways: A quantum computer can only do quantum computations with a maximum depth, MAXDEPTH, of 240 to 296. This is because creating a fully powerful quantum computer is extremely difficult. Moreover, that level of security is measured not in absolute time, but in the time required to complete a certain operation.

NIST offered six security levels as categories for the second agenda, each of which corresponds to a distinct task. For example, Category 1 entails finding the 128-bit key of an AES-128-based block cipher. For example, the security level inferred for AES-256 is that it is as difficult to break by quantum computers as it is for a classical computer to break AES-128. And that is why AES-256 is quantum-safe. To have an idea of what this is, breaking the AES-128 cipher by checking each of the 2128 conceivable key values (employing a brute force assault) is so computationally intensive that it would take the fastest classical supercomputer over 100 trillion years to decipher!

 As a result, AES is difficult to break (if not compromised), and it is projected to remain safe for many years based on current technology trends. AES is the encryption algorithm of choice for governments, financial institutions, and security organizations all around the world. That is why the National Security Agency (NSA) in the United States employs it to protect the country's top-secret data.

3.3 Homophonic Encryption

As shown in the experiment section, a substitution cipher can be constructed by assigning homophones to entire plaintexts, such as words and phrases. Substitution ciphers often have the following features: plaintext, a codebook containing the dictionary, and tokens. For example,

Plaintext: I am going to the market to buy some tomatoes for my wife to cook dinner tonight

Dictionary list: [am1 buy2 cook3 dinner4 for5 going6 I7 market8 my9 some10 the11 to12 tomatoes13 tonight14 wife15].

Note that the word to occurs three times in the plaintext but is listed only once in the dictionary! The matrix created by substituting the position of occurrences in the plaintext looks like this:

[(1(2)), (2(8)), (3(15)), (4(16)), (5(11)), (6(3)), (7(1)), (8(6)),

(9(12)), (10(9)), (11(5)), (12(4,7,14)), (13(10)), (14(17)), (15(13))]

These are the positions of each word in the dictionary paired with the occurrences in the plaintext. Did you notice that the word to is 12th in the dictionary list in the matrix, and occurs three times in the plaintext at positions 4, 7, 14, so it is denoted as (12(4,7,14))? This matrix can be used to reconstruct the plaintext verbatim from the dictionary. However, during the research, it was found that the substitution matrix can be subjected to frequency analysis. Therefore, to avoid frequency analysis, each repeated occurrence of a text such as "to" should be replaced by homophones (unique tokens) to cause frequency flattening. The essence of homophonic ciphering is to effectively thwart frequency attacks, which is the only known weakness of substitution ciphers.

Using the homophonic technique to cipher databases is an effective way to give owners confidence that their digital assets will not be compromised, because it is like looking at a black box when you do not have access to the codebook. This is why the homophonic substitution technique should greatly be used for data protection in PQC systems.

3.4 Identity Based Encryption (NTRU)

What is a Lattice? A lattice is a structure that can be defined mathematical as a discrete subgroup of real numbers Rn, such that for the point v of vector space over a finite field F, all possible linear combinations of the basis B with integer coefficients (where ai ? F, bi ? B) will be the lattice:

  L = {v = a1b1 + a2b2 + ... + anbn : ai ? F, bi ? B}

This IBE scheme uses the NTRU and the Ring-LWE assumption, which is considered resistant to quantum attacks. The NTRU uses a lattice-based approach, so to break it using the closest vector problem (CVP), we need to find the point in the space closest to a lattice point. This is NP-hard and the shortest vector problem (SVP) is also NP-hard under randomized reductions. The truncated polynomial ring of the scheme is given as:

 Ring: R = Zp[X]/(XM 1) Secret key: (f,g) ? R2

 public key:  h=g/f (mod p)

Where M is the power of 2, p is a prime congruent of 1 mod 2M, so that the polynomial XM 1 is split to M linear factors over ZP.

The IBE scheme of NTRU is made up of 4 main algorithms namely:

Master KeyGen: which generates the master private Key and the master public key that has to do with the NTRU lattice.

Extract: This algorithm generates the users private key by their identity. It uses the master private key with a specified hash function to do this.

Encryption and Decryption: the encryption here uses a generic ring-LWE scheme. Normally, the encryption automatically processes some error in the polynomials to encapsulate the key. To decrypt the message, the key is recovered, rounding up in the DLP-IBE scheme, to make the message known.

4.4 The Validation

4.3.2  The Friedman frequency test

William Friedman invented the Friedman test, also called the Kappa test, in the 1920s to crack ciphers by using the coincidence index that measures the unevenness of cipher letter frequencies. A Friedman test can tell the difference between monoalphabetic and polyalphabetic encrypted text. The Kappa test Ic value ranges between 0.038 to 0.066. A value range of around 0.065 strongly indicates the use of monoalphabetic encryption (such as an affine cipher, basic substitution cipher, Caesar cipher, or keyword cipher), whereas a value of Ic near 0.038 indicates the use of a polyalphabetic cipher such as Vigenre cipher or homophonic. (Gobi & Kurma, 2018).

Monoalphabetic encryption can be broken more easily than polyalphabetic. At 0.038 it is a strong polyalphabetic, like homophonic encryption of whole texts (like words or phrases) that defeats frequency analysis completely. Below is the result conducted on homophonic ciphertext of 0.632KB, using the Cryptool tool Friedman test for classical ciphers to investigate the nature of unknown encryptions.

This research paper addresses the key management problem using IBE to bring the reliability of asymmetric key encryption for data exchange into our PQC schemes. The research also shows how we can conveniently protect our database from cryptanalysis using HE.

Another related work that inspired and supported this research is Cedric's research on IBE, which illustrated how a new architectural method of key management based on a PKI, Boneh, and Franklin IBE scheme will lead to effective certificate-less key management. (Cedric, 2016).

 The contribution for security using homophonic encryption is that in can be implemented to defeat cryptanalysis and protect our databases very effectively. Related research in this area by Lacharit & Paterson demonstrated how Frequency Smoothing Encryption (FSE) can be used for frequency-smoothing to prevent snapshot attacks on data that are deterministically encrypted and used in database protection to shield queries from inference attacks based on frequency analysis. (Lacharit & Paterson, 2018).

5.2 Summary and Future Works

Data breaches have become a problem worldwide, not only for enterprises but also for governments. The use of PQC schemes to protect sensitive data from unauthorized access is what some nations are embarking on. Although homophonic encryption is well known, it has received little or no attention and uses worldwide, which is why this research finding is interesting. The two ciphers recommended here are very promising, both as a theoretical model and a practical concept, since they can be used inexpensively and flexibly for private use at little cost. In conjunction with other algorithms from PQ, they have the potential to solve many functional problems and provide absolute data security.
